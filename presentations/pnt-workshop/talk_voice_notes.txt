
So fundamentally, with this toolkit I'm making, I'm trying to be really, really practical and just solve my own problem. The reason I'm doing that is because I have a pretty serious problem from time to time, and it requires talking to people who I can have a deep, meaningful conversation with. Sometimes those are hard to find, and the way to resolve that is to increase the total availability of people I can contact when help-seeking. 

So the first thing I would do when in a difficult situation is I would hop on LinkedIn. If the difficult situation has to do with depression, you do not have a lot of energy remaining for long, boring, menial tasks necessarily. Part way through scrolling through the letter A's, you don't really know any of these people, and you start to wonder about friendship.
The reasons for that are:
- LinkedIn is not designed to maximize your ability to help seek in a serious way.
- Facebook isn't either, so you end up with the same problem.
- Facebook is not set up primarily to maximize your ability to seek help or to give help.

And so this kind of leads you to the whole local first software community. It's just obvious that you need this stuff. The first thing that you do is you take a piece of paper and solve your problem with a piece of paper, and then you write down as many people as you can that you can contact for help. This is just a smaller exercise of a larger goal of having good social network health. Being a strength to your community and your community being a strength to you improves everyone's mental health outcomes.
